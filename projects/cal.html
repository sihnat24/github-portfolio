Overview

What it is & why

Protein-peptide binding affinity (Kd) measures how strongly a peptide binds to a target protein, and is a core quantity in computational drug discovery. Identifying which peptides bind to a given target has traditionally been a manual, low-throughput, and expensive process, making it difficult to explore large portions of the sequence space. Machine learning offers a way to predict these interactions computationally, allowing researchers to prioritize promising candidates before committing to experimental validation.

Protein domains are regions of a protein sequence where peptides tend to bind, and both can be represented as sequences of amino acids. Domains vary considerably in length, composition, and the types of sequences that bind to them, making each domain its own distinct prediction problem. The data available for any given domain also tends to be highly repetitive, as experimental studies often test the same domains and peptides, meaning that simply collecting more labeled examples does not always translate to more useful information.

Some protein domains are far better studied than others, with large bodies of experimental data available, while others remain relatively unexplored. If a model could learn to predict binding affinity in a general sense, researchers would not need to separately gather data and train dedicated models for each new domain they encounter. A model that generalizes well across domains could extend the benefits of computational screening to targets where experimental data is sparse, broadening the scope of what is tractable in early stage drug discovery.

This project investigates active learning techniques, which aim to reduce the number of labeled samples needed for training by selecting the most informative examples, alongside continual learning methods, which address the problem of forgetting. This research explores whether combining these two techniques can produce models that generalize across domains without requiring large amounts of labeled data for each new one encountered.

Technical Approach

Architecture & decisions

The binding affinity predictor is built on an architecture adapted from a published model, extended to support generalization across multiple protein domains. The overall training loop is illustrated in the figure below.

The approach begins with a model pretrained on one domain, alongside two separate data pools: one for the new target domain and one for the original domain. Each round, an acquisition function scores and selects the most informative samples from each pool, with the target domain samples chosen to guide adaptation and the original domain samples serving as replay data to help the model retain prior knowledge. The selected samples are combined with all previously selected data to update the model weights, then removed from their respective pools, and the process repeats.

Experiments vary parameters such as acquisition strategy, training duration, and the proportion of new versus replay data, and more, all run on Columbia's high performance computing cluster.

Results

Current Observations

Experiments are ongoing. Early results point to an interesting trade-off: adapting to a new domain tends to reduce quantitative affinity prediction accuracy, while binary classification of binders versus non-binders improves on the new domain.

Current work is focused on understanding how budget allocation and acquisition strategy influence this trade-off, with the goal of finding configurations that generalize well across domains.
