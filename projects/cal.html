Overview

What it is & why

Protein-peptide binding affinity (Kd) measures how strongly a peptide binds to a target protein, and is a core quantity in computational drug discovery. Experimental measurement is expensive and time consuming, making it valuable to have models that can predict affinity from sequence alone.

The models trained on a single protein domain tend to perform well within that domain, but struggle when applied to new ones. Naively extending training to cover new domains degrades performance, so more principled approaches to generalization are needed. Tools that can reliably predict binding across many protein targets could broadly accelerate research in any domain where protein interactions play a role, offering a computationally cheap first pass before committing to expensive experimental validation.

This project explores continual active learning as a framework for adapting strong single-domain models to new protein domains, acquiring as few new labels as possible in the process. The GYF and WH1 domains serve as the primary testbed.

Technical Approach

Architecture & decisions

The binding affinity predictor is built on an architecture adapted from a published model, extended to support generalization across multiple protein domains.

As illustrated in the figure below, the approach starts from a model already trained on one domain and fine-tunes it on a new target domain using active learning, which prioritizes the most informative new samples for labeling. At the same time, continual learning exposes the model to replay data from its original domain, helping it retain prior knowledge. Samples from both pools are selected either greedily or at random each round.

Experiments sweep over acquisition strategy, training duration, and the proportion of new versus replay data, all run on Columbia's high performance computing cluster.

Results

Current Observations

Experiments are ongoing. Early results point to an interesting trade-off: adapting to a new domain tends to reduce quantitative affinity prediction accuracy, while binary classification of binders versus non-binders improves on the new domain.

Current work is focused on understanding how budget allocation and acquisition strategy influence this trade-off, with the goal of finding configurations that generalize well across domains.
